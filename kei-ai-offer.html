Designing For Reality

by Paul Cloutier
July 13, 2024

Paul Cloutier

Paul Cloutier is a leader in designing businesses. As an entrepreneur–both on the client side and the agency side–Paul has helped some of the world’s most effective brands bring experiences to life.

More often than not–after spending a great deal of time, money, and precious focus–a product or service launch gets dropped into the world with a well advertised thud. All of the things were done to ensure it’s success, the best designers and engineers, the consultancy that looked at the market and talked to customers, but somehow, something was missed.

In many of these cases failure has come because we are designing for the parameters that we want rather than the parameters that are actually there. Either we intentionally ignored details that were inconvenient, or we didn’t have the data from the right people and places–we didn’t design for reality, we designed for a fantasy that we wanted to be reality.

What is generally missing is a deep enough consideration of the parts that get left out of the design process, a bringing together of people, and a full considered conversation rather than a siloed set of separate discussions. The magic happens when we are all working, talking, and brainstorming together with as much information as possible.

Sometimes these projects fail because product development is just not the core skillset of the company, they don’t have the organizational readiness. The challenge though is that if you want to operate in a modern service led world you have to have this skill as a core competency and can’t hire it out forever. the miss from an agency standpoint is finding ways to monetize making yourself irrelevant. many of these organizations have no skill at hiring designers or strategists, they have no management capability to integrate them in ways that will keep those creative workers happy, and most importantly they don’t have the executive resilience or design maturity to do this work and so the product fails.

And this process isn’t just about getting the right people at the table, it is also reckoning with the actual cost of things. Many times businesses suffer because they don’t realize how much more things costs than what is advertised on the proposal. The design and engineering are just the tip of the iceberg. Designing for reality means understanding these hidden costs, and building them into your planning and operation. How many people will need to run it, how will they be trained, how will you get them involved early enough that they don’t reject the thinking. And of course this understanding the externalized costs means understanding the downstream implications of your work, we can no longer afford to leave the impacts of our decisions to the next generation

Consulting has worked to build processes of co-design, user centered thinking, and business design to tangle with these issues, recognizing that product excellence comes not just from the artifacts, but from how that product is operationalized into providing a good experience. The general approach here has been making a bigger table to get more folks around it. This is an admirable strategy but inherently unscalable, and requires an almost religious level of ongoing dedication to the process on the part of the client, without any of the training and skillset to do so.

Another challenge has often been that the consultancies and the client are often working at cross purposes, and from different first principles–a divorce of corp strategy and product strategy. The principles that are driving the product thinking quite often either ignore the business realities, the customer realities, or the simple reality that people in a company don’t generally like to be told what to do by outsiders. And on the client side, the principles driving their decision making often have many other products and business lines to consider, legal issues, shareholder management, training, ops, and so many other areas, that the product decision making is left to more tactical layers of a company, making true strategic decision making unlikely and myopic at best.

They answer for us has been purpose-led business thinking, what started as an extension of brand experience–the alignment of all of your customer touch-points with your brand values–has led to a more extended framework that takes into account your goals, values, mission and principles and harnesses them for strategic business decision making.

This purpose led business thinking defines a companies purpose in an actionable format, it understands the fundamentals of the business reality and the customer reality, while creating a working framework that can give guidance at every step. This purpose led framework can inform the brand strategy, the product strategy, the growth strategy, the operations strategy, and the marketing strategy–theoretically everything a business needs to make a decision about.

Turning the Tables

What starts to become compelling about the changing nature of design and strategy consultancy is the role that vast amounts of data can play. When we talk about designing with AI, of course this means designing new types of interfaces for AI products, but much more importantly it means infusing AI and LLMs and these new agents into our design processes. The new insights, the new angles, the totality of information, the evolutionary strategies, the infinite iterations, all will help us leapfrog the unwieldy process of “making a bigger and bigger table”

A Note about Designing for Reality

Designing for reality means taking everything that you can into consideration, working with all of the parts of the organization, accounting for the actual, total costs of doing business, and aligning your mission vision and values to what you actually do as a business. The alternative to this is just going down a list of best practices checking boxes like a cargo cult, talking about values without actually delivering on them, creating design artifacts that never move beyond dropbox or a local staging server.

There are several key ingredients that we are working with here:

01.
Collaboration

The fullness of communi-cation, of bringing people together, of bringing the right people together, of building teams that are right for the project, of finding perspectives that are often ignored or left out of the process.

02.
Purpose

Understanding why we are doing what we are doing and finding ways to drive that purpose through every aspect of the org, finding ways to make sure we deliver of the promise we have made, and recognizing that that purpose gives you permission to enter new and interesting areas.

03.
AI as a design process

LLMs, computational creativity, and simulated Agents can provide hidden insights, evolutionary prototyping, mass customization, and asset generation, unlocking totally new approaches that weren’t available previously.

A big part of understanding systems thinking is seeing the hidden connections and this is where we use data and agents to make that system more visible and workable.

04.
Craft

Probably worth noting craft as a component as well considering who we are and where we come from. We aren’t just thinkers, we are also makers, we can bring things to life. The awareness of how things are made and the mastery of the tools gives us an encyclopedic knowledge of possibilities and approaches.

A Note About Regenerative Design

This direction isn’t about shifting away from regenerative design per se, it's about sharpening the offer and putting its value in the right place. One of the stated concerns about the positioning of Regenerative is that it places us in a tier of the org that often has less money, less power, and regularly working on projects that have a high potential for greenwashing.

The new approach that we are working on places regenerative design as a core capability of how we work, rather than as a market segment. Regardless of the client and regardless of their enthusiasm, we can layer this more holistic approach into our work. One of the key ways we can do this is through the full accounting of external costs on our projects. as a key part of our framework we can help explore energy costs, people costs, work style, location, materials and many other aspects and possibly have more of an impact than we might if we came in the front door trumpeting our regenerative bonafides.

This also doesn’t preclude us from targeting climate, energy, or human scale projects, these can still be verticals and market segments we target and develop thought leadership in.
