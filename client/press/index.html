The Place

A Building That Remembers

The former Allen Press site in East Lawrence carried an idea in its bones for decades. This was a place where people came to work with their hands and their minds together, where raw material went in and something real came out, where the act of production was collective and purposeful. The buildings remember that even if the machines are gone.

A complex with the variety and scale to hold many kinds of making under one roof.

The Idea

Bell Labs for Makers

Bell Labs worked because it put physicists next to engineers next to mathematicians and trusted that proximity and collision would produce things none of them could have made alone. The breakthroughs came from the in-between spaces.

That logic, applied to the full messy spectrum of creative and making life in a mid-sized American city, is what The Press is built on.

This is a dirty hands place. Inky, greasy, clay dusty, saw dusty. Welding and blacksmithing live here alongside 3D printing, arduino, video production, and letterpress. There is no hierarchy between those things.

The Framework

Three Poles, One Ecosystem

The physical complexity of the Allen Press site already suggests how the space wants to be used. The variety reads as an asset. Different buildings suit different kinds of activity, and that natural differentiation maps onto three distinct but complementary poles. The poles are layers of a single ecosystem, each reinforcing the others.

The Curiosity Commons

Public-Facing · Drop-In · Community Access

The open, come-as-you-are layer. This is the soul of The Press. The animating question: what if anyone in Lawrence could come here and try something new?

Access is the organizing principle. Sliding scale membership, open studio time, drop-in programming, youth partnerships. The spaces are forgiving, flexible, and built for beginners as much as for serious practitioners.

The Entrepreneurial Campus

Serious Infrastructure · Creative Manufacturing

The infrastructure layer for makers and creative businesses who need real space and real equipment to do real work. The Kansas City region lacks a genuine creative manufacturing incubator at this scale, and that gap represents real tenant demand and meaningful economic development opportunity.

The large clear-span warehouse spaces with loading dock access are particularly well suited to this use.

The Operating Model

From Coffee to Community

The three poles create a natural pathway from casual exposure to deep participation. Someone walks in for coffee, watches a ceramicist through the studio window, signs up for a Saturday workshop, buys a monthly membership for bench time, rents a studio, and eventually launches a business from the campus.

That continuum from curiosity to commitment is the operating model. Every level of engagement has a home, and moving from one to the next feels natural rather than requiring a leap.

The Operating Philosophy

More Than a Landlord

The Press operates as an active partner to its tenants and the broader community. The model is closer to a community development organization than a venture capital firm. The goal is to stimulate the maker and creative economy, to put resources and infrastructure in the hands of people who will use them to build something real.

Capital & Business Support

Revolving loan fund for critical growth moments. CDFI and SBA connections. Shared bookkeeping, HR templates, legal resources, and insurance pooling to reduce overhead for small operators.

Knowledge & Community

Programming calendar of workshops, talks, critiques, and open houses. Mentorship networks. Cross-tenant project brokering. A residency program bringing outside makers in for defined periods.

Shared Infrastructure

Equipment libraries beyond individual studios. Freight and logistics support using the loading docks. High-quality shared internet. A Press membership for non-tenants who want lighter access.

Neighborhood Commitment

Active participation in East Lawrence community organizations. Below-market studio rates. Youth programming partnerships with Lawrence public schools. Public events woven into the neighborhood.

Held Lightly

Live-Work as Mission

A direction worth exploring rather than a resolved part of the program.

The Allen Press site could support roughly 100 units, built new on a portion of the site. A 20,000 SF footprint with maker/commercial ground floor and five residential floors above.

The more specific version connects housing directly to the mission. The early Y Combinator model put founders in close physical proximity during the most formative period of building something. A live-work component oriented toward makers, artists, and early-stage creative entrepreneurs would be a genuine differentiator.

Residents become the most committed regulars, the ones who keep the coffee bar busy on a Wednesday morning and the ones most likely to cross the line from participant to maker to tenant.

The Neighborhood

The Neighborhood This Comes From

East Lawrence is a resilient, creative, working neighborhood. It sits directly adjacent to the Lawrence Cultural District, the densest creative neighborhood in Kansas. The university is a constant source of talent, energy, and institutional partnership opportunities.

What East Lawrence has lacked is a destination that reflects the community's own identity, builds wealth locally, and draws people in without displacing those already here.

No creative manufacturing incubator at this scale exists between Kansas City and Topeka. The Press fills a genuine regional gap.

Below-market studio rates, youth programming partnerships, active participation in neighborhood organizations, and public events that keep the place woven into the community are commitments, not aspirations.

What Comes Next

Questions, Not Conclusions

This concept is a framework for conversation, not a finished plan. The questions that shape the next phase:

What spaces in the building are best suited to each pole? How does the physical layout suggest the gradient from public-facing to production-heavy?

Who are the anchor tenants that make each pole real? What commitments can be secured early to build momentum?

What does the phasing look like? What opens first, and what generates the early revenue and energy that funds subsequent buildout?

How does the residential component connect to the mission, and what does the financial modeling say about feasibility?

How does The Press stay accountable to East Lawrence as it grows?
