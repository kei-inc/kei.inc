Cocktail Fundamentals

Tools, Ratios & Technique

The Tools

What you need behind the bar

The Shaker

The Boston shaker: a metal tin and a mixing glass (or two tins). Simple, professional, effective.

Why Two Pieces?

The seal forms from temperature differential—metal contracts as it chills, gripping the glass.

The Alternative

Cobbler shakers (three-piece with built-in strainer) work fine for home use but can freeze shut.

The Strainer

A Hawthorne strainer fits over your shaker tin. The coiled spring catches ice and large particles.

Hawthorne

The workhorse. Spring coil, flat face, fits shaker tins. Press the spring tighter for finer straining.

Julep Strainer

Perforated bowl shape. Traditional for stirred drinks—fits elegantly into a mixing glass.

The Jigger

Precision matters. A cocktail is a small thing—a quarter ounce off throws the whole balance.

Japanese Style

Tall, slim, with interior gradations. More precise, easier to read, elegant to use.

Standard Double

1oz / 2oz hourglass. Simple, fast. Know your pours: fill to the brim for accuracy.

Pro tip

Pour with the jigger close to your mixing vessel. Lifting it high looks theatrical but loses liquid to drips.

Mixing Glass & Bar Spoon

For stirred drinks. Heavy glass holds cold; the spoon's spiral shaft lets you rotate smoothly.

The Glass

Thick-walled, heavy base. The weight keeps it stable. A pint glass works in a pinch.

The Spoon

Long, twisted shaft. Hold it between fingers, let the spiral guide rotation against the glass wall.

The Ingredients

Building blocks of every cocktail

Base Spirits

The foundation. Usually 1.5–2oz, providing the drink's primary character and backbone.

Whiskey

Bourbon (sweet, corn), Rye (spicy, grain), Scotch (smoky, malty)

Gin

Juniper-forward. London Dry is classic; modern styles range from floral to citrus

Rum

White (clean, mixing), Aged (vanilla, caramel), Agricole (grassy, funky)

Others

Vodka (neutral), Tequila/Mezcal (agave), Brandy (grape), and beyond

Modifiers & Bitters

Modifiers

Vermouths (sweet, dry, blanc), liqueurs, and amari. They add complexity, sweetness, herbal notes, or bitterness.

Think of them as the supporting cast—they shape the drink's direction without overpowering the lead.

Bitters

Concentrated botanical extracts. A few dashes transform a drink. Angostura is essential; Peychaud's and orange bitters round out the basics.

Like salt in cooking—you don't taste them directly, but everything's flat without them.

Fresh & Sweet

Citrus

Always fresh-squeezed. Lemon and lime are the workhorses. Juice oxidizes fast—squeeze the same day you use it.

Bottled juice is dead juice. This is the one place where shortcuts kill the drink.

Sweeteners

Simple syrup (1:1 sugar to water) is the default. Rich syrup (2:1) for more body. Honey, maple, demerara—each brings character.

Sweetness balances acid and tempers alcohol heat. Get this ratio right, everything clicks.

The Technique

Shaking and stirring—when and how

When to Shake

Shake anything with citrus, juice, dairy, egg, or ingredients that don't want to combine.

1
Integration — Shaking forces disparate ingredients to emulsify. Citrus and spirits don't naturally mix; violence makes them cooperate.
2
Aeration — Shaking introduces tiny air bubbles, giving the drink a lighter, frothier texture.
3
Texture — That silky mouthfeel in a good sour? That's the shake creating it.

How to Shake

1
Load and seal — Ingredients in glass, ice in tin. Tap firmly to seal. Tin on top, angled slightly.
2
Full motion — Shake from the shoulder, not the wrist. The whole arm moves. Hard, deliberate, horizontal.
3
10–12 seconds — That's it. The tin will be frost-cold. Longer just adds dilution without more chill.
4
Break and strain — Heel of hand on the tin's side where it meets the glass. Pop, strain, serve.

The Science

You're doing three things at once: chilling (to ~35°F), diluting (adding 20–25% water), and aerating. The water isn't a flaw—it's essential. It opens up flavors and softens the alcohol.

When & How to Stir

Stir spirit-forward drinks with no citrus. You want cold and dilution—but not aeration. The texture should stay silky, not frothy.

1
Ice first — Fill the mixing glass with ice. Add your ingredients.
2
Stir gently — Spoon against the glass wall, smooth rotations. No clinking, no splashing. 30–40 rotations.
3
Strain clean — Julep strainer for mixing glass, Hawthorne for tin. Into a chilled coupe or over fresh ice.

The Goal

Same chill and dilution as shaking, but preserving clarity and that dense, viscous texture that makes a Manhattan or Martini feel substantial.

The Drinks

One shaken, one stirred

Gold Rush

A modern classic. The honey adds warmth and body—perfect for winter.

Shaken

  • 2 oz Bourbon
  • ¾ oz Fresh lemon juice
  • ¾ oz Honey syrup (1:1 honey + hot water)

Shake hard with ice. Strain into a rocks glass over fresh ice. No garnish needed—maybe a lemon twist if you're feeling fancy.

The Honey Syrup

Equal parts honey and hot water, stirred until dissolved. Raw honey won't incorporate properly in cold liquid—the syrup solves that.

The Hanmattan

A reverse Manhattan—vermouth-forward, lighter, sessionable. All the elegance, easier on the pour.

Stirred

  • 2 oz Sweet vermouth
  • 1 oz Bourbon (or rye)
  • 2 dashes Angostura bitters
  • 1 Cherry or orange peel

Combine in a mixing glass with ice. Stir 30–40 rotations until well-chilled. Strain into a chilled coupe. Garnish with a cherry or express an orange peel.

Why It Works

Flipping the ratio lets the vermouth's botanicals lead while the bourbon provides warmth and structure. Lower ABV, more complex aromatics, very approachable.
