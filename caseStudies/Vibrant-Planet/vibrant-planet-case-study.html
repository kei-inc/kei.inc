Reshaping Forest Management: Kei's Collaboration with Vibrant Planet on Land Tender

Introduction

In the face of escalating climate change and increasingly devastating wildfires, the need for innovative approaches to forest management has never been more critical. This case study delves into Kei's multi-year collaboration with Vibrant Planet to develop Land Tender, a groundbreaking platform that is revolutionizing how we approach ecological resilience and wildfire mitigation.

The Challenge: A Burning Crisis

California's forests were at a critical juncture. In 2020 alone, wildfires resulted in staggering losses:

  • $3-4 trillion in ecosystem benefit losses at current market valuation
  • $63 billion in public health impacts
  • $2.3 billion in fire suppression costs
  • 20 years of lost profitability for the insurance industry due to structure damage and loss

With the potential loss of a large portion of California's forests looming in the next 15-20 years, urgent action was needed. However, the complexity of the issue demanded a nuanced approach. Not all fires are detrimental, and not every tree contributes positively to forest health. The consequences of inaction were dire: increased mass mortality, severe climate impacts, water scarcity, disruption of California's food system, and significant threats to human health.

The challenges were multifaceted:

  • Fragmented data and decision-making processes across numerous agencies and stakeholders
  • Lack of real-time, high-resolution data on forest health and fire risk
  • Slow and cumbersome planning processes for forest management projects
  • Difficulty in visualizing and communicating complex forest management scenarios
  • Balancing multiple objectives like fire risk reduction, habitat preservation, and carbon sequestration

Kei's Approach: Systems Thinking in Action

Recognizing the multifaceted nature of the challenge, Kei's team, led by Kevin Farnham, approached the project with a comprehensive, systems-thinking methodology:

  • Comprehensive Stakeholder Engagement

    The team conducted over 100 interviews, contextual inquiries, and co-design sessions across a broad spectrum of stakeholders, including:

    • Land managers from the US Forest Service and The Nature Conservancy
    • Calfire's top management
    • Scientists with expertise in forest ecology, wildfire, hydrology, wildlife habitat, and meteorology
    • Emergency responders
    • Policy makers
    • Industry representatives
  • Identifying Core Needs

    Through this extensive research, several key requirements emerged:

    • Scalable, reliable, high-resolution data layers consolidated in one place
    • User-friendly planning and predictive decision support tools
    • Easier collaboration between team members and agencies
    • Comprehensive, timely monitoring and progress tracking
    • A shift from 75% of resources spent on suppression to 75% spent on restoration
    • Tools to empower teams to do planning and prioritization with science-based decision support
  • Multi-scale Design

    The team recognized the need for a solution that could operate at various scales:

    • Global and national levels for policy making
    • State and regional levels for large-scale planning
    • Local and property levels for specific interventions

    This multi-scale approach ensures that Land Tender can provide valuable insights and tools for decision-makers at all levels.

  • AI and Data Integration

    Leveraging cutting-edge AI and machine learning techniques to:

    • Process and analyze vast amounts of environmental data
    • Keep forest maps current and accurate
    • Provide predictive modeling for various scenarios
  • User-Centric Design

    Focusing on creating an intuitive interface that various stakeholders, from policymakers to on-the-ground forest managers, could easily use and understand.

The Solution: Land Tender

Land Tender emerged as a cloud-based natural resource management platform designed to accelerate and scale the restoration of resilience to rapidly changing ecological systems. Key features include:

  • Comprehensive Data Integration
    • Consolidation of high-resolution data on vegetation, topography, infrastructure, and more
    • Integration of various data sources, including LiDAR, satellite imagery, and ground-based observations
    • Regular updates to maintain current, accurate information
  • AI-Driven Analysis
    • Machine learning algorithms to map forests at an unprecedented fine scale
    • Continuous updating of forest maps to reflect changes in real-time
    • Predictive modeling to forecast potential outcomes of different management strategies
  • Scenario Modeling
    • Real-time scenario generation to understand risk and prioritize treatments
    • Ability to model long-term impacts of different management decisions
    • Incorporation of climate change projections into scenario planning
  • Collaborative Tools
    • Facilitation of cooperation between various stakeholders
    • Shared workspaces for multi-agency projects
    • Tools for public engagement and education
  • Adaptive Management
    • Real-time updates and adjustments as conditions change or new data becomes available
    • Monitoring tools to track the effectiveness of interventions over time
  • Decision Support System
    • AI-enhanced optimization for treatment types and locations
    • Tools for weighing trade-offs between different management objectives
    • Integration of economic, ecological, and social factors in decision-making
  • Visualization Tools
    • Advanced 3D visualization of forest landscapes
    • Before-and-after comparisons of management interventions
    • Interactive maps for stakeholder engagement

Development Challenges and Adaptations

The journey to create Land Tender was not without its hurdles:

  • Stakeholder Changes

    Midway through the project, there was a significant shakeup among the stakeholders, with some key people leaving the project. This required the team to adapt their approach and maintain project momentum despite the changes.

  • Funding Challenges

    In the early stages, securing funding proved difficult. Allison Wolff, CEO of Vibrant Planet, demonstrated remarkable resourcefulness by piecing together funding from various public and private sources to keep the project moving forward.

  • Evolving Requirements

    As the project progressed and new stakeholders came on board, the team had to remain flexible, continually adapting the platform to meet emerging needs and expectations.

  • Data Integration

    Combining diverse data sources with varying resolutions and update frequencies posed significant technical challenges.

  • Balancing Complexity and Usability

    Creating a system sophisticated enough to model complex ecological systems while remaining user-friendly required constant iteration and user testing.

Impact and Outcomes

Land Tender has shown promising results and attracted significant investment:

  • Tahoe Basin Deployment

    The first major customer installation covers 1.5 million acres across the Lake Tahoe Basin and surrounding critical watersheds, including the Middle Truckee River, American River, Bear River, Feather River, and Yuba River watersheds.

  • Significant Funding

    In 2022, Vibrant Planet secured $17M in initial funding. Recently, they raised an additional $15M in a Series A round led by the Ecosystem Integrity Fund, bringing their total funding to $34 million.

  • Corporate Adoption

    PG&E, a utility responsible for 70,000 square miles of wildfire-prone landscapes, has signed on as a customer, marking a significant step into the corporate sector.

  • Expanding Coverage

    Land Tender is now being used to manage 7 million acres, an area larger than the state of Vermont.

  • Paradigm Shift

    The platform is changing how land managers approach forest resilience, moving from reactive fire suppression to proactive ecosystem management.

  • Growing Team

    Vibrant Planet has assembled an impressive science team, including experts from academia, the U.S. Forest Service, Bureau of Land Management, and NASA.

  • Expanding Reach

    While California was the initial focus, Land Tender has already been implemented in other regions, demonstrating its adaptability and potential for widespread application in various fire-prone ecosystems.

Lessons Learned

  • The Power of Systems Thinking: By considering the entire ecosystem of forest management, from policy to on-the-ground implementation, Kei created a solution that addresses root causes rather than symptoms.
  • User-Centric Design is Crucial: The extensive stakeholder engagement process ensured that Land Tender met real needs and would be adopted by its intended users.
  • AI as an Enabler: By leveraging AI and machine learning, Land Tender can process vast amounts of data and provide insights that would be impossible for humans alone.
  • Balancing Complexity and Usability: While the underlying system is highly complex, the team focused on creating an intuitive interface that various stakeholders could easily use.
  • Adaptability is Key: The ability to pivot and adapt to changing stakeholder needs and emerging challenges was crucial to the project's success.
  • Interdisciplinary Collaboration: The project's success hinged on bringing together experts from various fields, including ecology, data science, UX design, and policy.

The Future

As climate change continues to impact our forests globally, tools like Land Tender will become increasingly crucial. Vibrant Planet's vision extends beyond California, aiming to create a comprehensive platform for ecological resilience that can be applied to fire-prone ecosystems worldwide.

The potential applications of Land Tender are vast:

  • Expanding to other types of ecosystems beyond forests
  • Integration with climate change mitigation and adaptation strategies
  • Supporting sustainable urban planning in wildland-urban interface areas
  • Informing policy decisions at local, state, and national levels

By bridging the gap between cutting-edge technology and on-the-ground forest management, Kei and Vibrant Planet have created a powerful tool for shaping a more resilient future for our forests and communities. Land Tender stands as a testament to the power of innovative thinking, interdisciplinary collaboration, and user-centered design in addressing some of the most pressing environmental challenges of our time.

2018–2020

Background

Today’s intense, destructive, and costly wildfires are driven by unnaturally dense forests, expanded development in fire-prone areas, and climate change, which has dried out vegetation, extended the fire season, and led to pest outbreaks that have killed millions of trees across the western U.S.

In California in 2020 alone, wildfires resulted in:

  • $3-4 trillion in ecosystem benefit losses at today’s market valuation, including carbon emissions and loss of carbon sequestration, water quality, recreation, and wood products
  • $63 billion in public health impacts
  • $2.3 billion in fire suppression costs
  • 20 years of lost profitability for the insurance industry due to structure damage and loss.

With the potential loss of a large portion of California's forests in the next 15-20 years, it is crucial that we take action to restore them.

But the issue is complex – not all fires are bad and not all trees are good.

The consequences of inaction are dire: increased mass mortality, severe impacts on the climate, water scarcity, disruption of the California food system, and a significant threat to human health. To address the matter, it is necessary to engage policymakers, industries, funders, and the general public.

Our initial over-arching goal for this endeavor was to find ways to help increase the pace and scale of forest restoration in California (and eventually, the rest of the world). Our initial thinking about how we might achieve this task was to combine state-of-the-art spatial data (LIDAR), ultra-high-resolution satellite data, and user-friendly interfaces to make sense of the data. Although not incorrect, the needs were actually much more profound.

Research

Our primary interest was to understand the pain points and needs of various constituents involved in California land / forest / fire management to understand how specifically devised technology might help them. We created a far-reaching stakeholder engagement process to understand workflows, tools used, core needs, pain points, and imagined use cases.

We conducted over 100 interviews, contextual inquiries, and co-design sessions across a broad cross-section of governmental, scientific, industry, land management, and emergency communities. Participants included land managers at the US Forest Service, The Nature Conservancy, Calfire’s top management, and scientists with expertise in forest ecology, wildfire, hydrology, wildlife habitat, and meteorology.

What We Heard

Current State

  • Scalable, reliable, high-resolution data layers in one place, collated, normalized, synthesized, cross-checked, and dynamically updated
  • Low resolution, questionably accurate vegetation and fuels data, updated every few years
  • Accessible, user-friendly planning, predictive decision support tools; 'SimCity-like' scenario building - weighing tradeoffs between impacts and benefits of land use decisions
  • Siloed and aging tools to support decision-making, none user-friendly
  • Easier collaboration between team members and agencies – leading to streamlined planning & documentation
  • 6 yr avg to move a project from planning to implementation
  • Comprehensive, timely monitoring and progress tracking informed by high temporal and spatial resolution remote sensing
  • Limited, empirically based monitoring
  • 75% of resources spent on suppression, 100k acres treated per year
  • 75% of resources spent on restoration, 3 million acres restored per year
  • Teams empowered to do planning and prioritization with science-based decision support; location-specific AI-enhanced optimization and treatment types
  • Significant skill (GIS, modeling) and time gaps (time to solutions)
  • Clear definitions of what conditions support long-term resilience and how to measure
  • Lack of consensus on what resiliency looks like
  • Mechanisms to help build public trust; Easier ways to educate stakeholders and the general public
  • Stakeholder and public education challenges
  • A common collaboration platform with easy to understand visualizations and easily accessed dashboard views
  • Need to support a broad range of stakeholders

Needs at Various Scales

What we began to understand is that there is a fundamental need for dynamic, scalable, synchronous data and decision making at every scale. Our revised goal was morphing to include guiding alignment across agencies, organizations, and the entire range of stakeholders – from government officials down to individual homeowners.

At different scales, the issues, stakeholders, timelines, budgets, visibility, and processes shift. Addressing these differences and varying needs at the UI and data visualization layers pose interesting challenges.

User Ecosystem

As we engaged with important stakeholders throughout this process, our comprehension of crucial user categories started to broaden and reveal their interconnections. To ensure the efficacy of our system, we realized the need to generate value for each group, which in turn would also motivate their participation through each other's involvement.

A Framework for Resilience

During our collaboration with the US Forest Service, we discovered that a diverse multi-agency team of scientists, land managers, and policy makers had been collaborating for years to develop a conceptual framework for resilience. The team aimed to provide a comprehensive perspective on land management that considered a range of desired benefits stemming from resilient socio-ecological systems. As part of their efforts, they identified ten key landscape outcomes, referred to as the "pillars of resilience".

The metrics underlying this framework are extensive, interconnected, and profound. We collaborated with their team to extend the thinking into our Elemental decision support system. We additionally incorporated the framework by co-developing a 'resilience scorecard' that users can employ to evaluate landscape metrics at multiple scales.

We created this visualization to help people understand the framework 'at-a-glance'. The visualization is also currently in use by the USFS.

Land Tender – Product UI Concept

Personal Workspaces

The Workspace is a easily accessed destination for:

  • All of the Projects a user is involved in
  • Communication and messages from other team members
  • News & Events compiled automatically based on project locations
  • A Library to research terminology, articles and papers

Project Workspaces

  • Quick, easy access to project maps
  • A Progress illustration for an ‘at-a-glance’ understanding of project status
  • Next Steps – a to-do list customized for team members
  • Updates - give users a quick understanding of what's been accomplished most recently
  • The Documents area contains both Elemental-created artifacts and other imported files
  • A Monitor shows recent land-based activity in the area of interest
  • A Media section provides automatically compiled social media posts, papers, and articles
  • A Team area for reference and quick messaging

Assess

The Assess area is geared to understanding, evaluating and viewing the conditions of the land. It is a combination of dashboards, resilience metrics, and data layers.

Dashboards are generated utilizing the Framework for Resilience and are automatically generated by the Elemental system.

Resilience Metrics utilize the Framework for Resilience to give a sense of AOI health at various scales.

Data Layers include individual data sets as well as Elemental derived and interpreted layers.

Watershed Scale

Relience Contribution Scores at this scale (and smaller) provide additional metrics and condition granularity.

Prioritize

The Prioritization area is comprised of three areas:

  • The Objectives area allows users and teams to create and refine objectives for the Area Of Interest.
  • Decision Spaces shows users the areas where management decisions are most needed on the map.
  • The Benefit Optimization functionality allows for individual benefits to be dialed up and down and shows what trade-offs might need to be made as a result.

In the objectives area, stakeholders are able to 'force rank' priorities for an AOI. The Elemental system uses artificial intelligence (and / or policy-based directives) to create an initial set of objectives based on a set of ecologically sound principles combined with an automatic analysis of the landscape. Team members can then 'drag' priorities up or down to better suit their specific circumstances or mandates.

Benefit Optimization

The Benefit Optimization area provides a slider-based interface to allow users to modify specific benefit metrics while seeing (in real time) the effects of those modifications on other resilience scores.

Detailed Optimizations

An example of detailed levels of granularity within the Optimization process. This screen shows a stakeholder who was invoked the Structure Detail panel within Forest Resilience to fine tune a potential treatment plan.

Analyze

The Analyze area of Elemental is focused on Modeling scenarios, understanding financial implications, and comparing scenarios to understand the benefits and potential effects over time.

The Modeling area allows users to build scenarios and understand potential effects of treatment plans. A time slider allows a user to view and analyze the treatment plan effects over the course of time. On this screen, Fire Risk is selected and modeled over a thirty year period.

Ecosystem Service Valuation in an Action vs. No Action Comparison

Stakeholders can quickly compare multiple treatment plans or no action scenarios in order to understand both the immediate tradeoffs as well as how those tradeoffs change over time.

Create

The Create area is focused on expediting communication of project plans through the use of templated documents, dashboards, Web pages, and presentations.

Document

A timeline is utilized for the creation of documents, Web pages, and presentations such as Power Point and Google Slides.

Team members can add many forms of Elemental data, upload images and add or customize text.

Build Dashboards

End users can create customized Elemental Dashboards for an AOI using a drag and drop UI. Saved elements are simply dragged into positions and customized to meet project needs.

These dashboards can then be easily revisited (with a direct Web address) on an ongoing basis and are automatically refreshed with the most current data.
